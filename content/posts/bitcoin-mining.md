When I first heard about Bitcoin back around in 2015, mining always sounded like
the coolest and most mysterious concept tied to the protocol. Mainstream media articles trying to explain
the process of mining read something like: "miners race to solve complex mathematical problems in order to validate the next block of transactions and earn a set amount of bitcoins as a reward".
This sounded cool and all but only added to the mystery and the confusion. What is a complex a mathematical problem?
Complex for who? Were those miners trying to come out with proofs to unsolved math puzzle 🤯? So many questions...

The answer is simpler than that. The type of complexity those miners are up against is called algorithmic complexity. In the case of bitcoin, miners are racing to solve a search problem, more specifically they are searching for a preimage of a set of numbers generated by the sha256 or Secure Hashing Algorithm 256.
A hash is an important type of algorithm used in cryptography that basically maps some arbitrary data into a number belonging to specific range of numbers.
This a fundamental property of all secure hashing algot is preimage resistant which means that it is computationally infeasible to find any input which hashes to a specific output. Since there is no efficient way of finding the preimage of a given number, miners essentially do random guesses, also called
a brute force search.
