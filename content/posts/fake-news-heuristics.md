Parsing through the incredible amount of data on the Web nowadays has never
been more challenging. Ranging from traditionnal news outlets
to posts found on social media, to scientific publications
from reputable journals or podcasts interviews and youtube videos,
information overload is ever present and filtering out the misinformation and
disinformation from the noise, specially if you are not a domain expert
is hard.

To help me navigate the maze of data on the Web I list here a set of
heuristics and general principles that I try to apply to my own thinking
and that have helped me, at least to some extent I believe from applying
faulty logic and falling victim to the plethora of cognitive biases to
which humans are subjected to.

I have chosen here to focus specifically on the Web due to its ubiquitousness
as a source of information in our daily lives and the present nature of Web
platforms which make heavy use of recommendation engines and as such make it
really easy to fall prey to the rabbit hole effect.

This is far from being an exhaustive or authoritive list, and is only intended
to provide a rough guideline for the busy consumer of content on the Internet.


### General and meta heuristics

definition of heuristics: method to approximate the solution to a problem
* misinformation/disinformation spreads faster than information
 this is a corrolary of the fact that truth is nuanced multifaceted complex, falsehoods are simple, appeal to human pattern matching preferences

A heuristic is a method to approximate an optimal solution to a problem.
Since the optimal solution usually requires some sort of costly computation,
specially if the search space is large we must use heuristics to reduce
computation time but still arrive at a solution that is close enough to the
optimal one. In the context of deciding whether or not some piece of information
found online is true or false, one might choose from a number of heuristics.
For example one could rely on expert opinion or on scientific consensus.
Heuristics are themselves a form of bias, in the previous examples expert
opinion might be construed as authority bias.

### heuristics directed to the parser, reader

* own cognitive biases (dunning kruger effect, sunk cost fallacy ...)
* be wary of the rabbit hole effect of recommendation engines


### heuristics directed to the messenger, deliver of information
(be careful not to fall for appeal to authority arguments)

* cognitive biases (same as for previous section apply here)
* logical and other fallacies from the MESSENGER
* use of jargon, vague or imprecise language, grandiose claims, too many metaphors 
* follow the ego, which defense mechanisms could be at play (without falling into an overly simplistic psychological model of the individual(s) involved
* guiding psyche traits of the messenger: arrogance vs humility
* tribalistic behaviour


### other considerations

* about conspiracy theories: always try to estimate the raw number of people that would have to be involved for the conspiracy to succeed, the more people the least likely the prpject is to succeed
* misinformation producers are louder and more numerous than experts who are busy and have no time to debunk false claims

### Closing remarks

* keep a healthy dose of skepcticism
